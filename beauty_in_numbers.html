Contrary to popular belief, math is beautiful

Math can express great meaning and fascinating imagery. Surely that means that math meets the qualifications of something that is beautiful, right?

Math, like poetry, can express beautiful imagery

Obviously beauty is subjective, however there are some things that we can almost all agree are beautiful in one way or another, such as a young child's laugh, or a truly moving piece of poetry. Another thing that we ought to add to such a list is math. Don't just believe me though, take a look for yourself.

That's a fractal, or more specifically, the Mandelbrot set. A fractal is a term in math that means, simply, something with properties that repeat forever. The Mandelbrot set, named after Benoit Mandelbrot, actually has some very complex math behind it, and when we use computers to visualize said math, it takes the form of the beautiful image above.

This is all very similar to poetry, believe it or not. In your middle school English class, you probably learned about something called "imagery." Imagery is a very powerful tool that allows poets to use the English language to craft beautiful visuals from words that magically appear in a readers' head. As Ron Aharoni says in Mathematics, Poetry, and Beauty, "mathematicians too, think in pictures," which allows them to create beautiful formulas that form things like the Mandelbrot set and more.

Great ideas are expressed in mathematics

While the visuals are truly spectacular, there's more to math than just that. I don't think that many of us have fond memories of trigonometry in high school. Most of us probably remember painstakingly learning that sine and cosine do ... something? With a circle? And some triangles? This is probably due to how trig is taught in schools, instead of being taught to recognize the beauty of what the famous trig functions do, we were taught to be afraid of the difficulty of memorizing tons of seemingly random and pointless identities. As Paul Lockhart puts it in A Mathematician's Lament, "our present system of mathematics education is ... a mechanism for the express purpose of destroying a child’s natural curiosity and love of pattern-marking."

In reality, trig functions actually carry great meaning. Without sine and cosine, bridges wouldn't be nearly as stable, buildings would rarely be more than simple cubes, and, possibly worst of all, most video games we know and love today wouldn't be possible. They are very beautifully defined functions that allow mathematicians and engineers to manipulate the real world in a truly unique way. All because, essentially, sine and cosine are the height and width of a triangle, respectively. Obviously math can get really complicated, but the fundamentals that we so often skip over and take for granted are arguably the most important and satisfying things to learn in math. These should be the things that teachers are incentivized to teach young students in order to create or uncover a passion for math in each of us.

Math can also carry complex ideas within itself and even make leaps between theoretical ideas, showing how beautiful and intertwined math can be. One particular identity that does just this is called Euler's identity, named after the great mathematician Leonhard Euler. Euler's identity, as science4all says, is how Euler "merged the most amazing numbers of mathematics" into one expression: eiπ + 1 = 0. This is considered beautiful because it manages to combine Euler's number, e, commonly found in economics and engineering, i, the imaginary number that is used in several advanced areas of math, π, the famous irrational and transcendental number that is the circumference of the unit circle, 1, the multiplicative identity, and 0 the additive identity. All of these numbers are independently useful and interesting, and the fact that there is a way to relate them all is truly beautiful.

"But there's no interpretation!"

Actually there is!

In fact, math can even be controversial. Bet you never considered that, huh? About a hundred years ago, a fantastic self-taught mathematician named Srinivasa Ramanujan declared that 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + ... = -1/12. Or in other words, if you add every positive whole number, it equals a negative fraction. That's not intuitive at all, and many people don't believe it, including mathematicians, but as the nature of controversial topics tends to go, there are also many mathematicians that do believe it. A content creator on YouTube named Mathologer has a great video on this famous statement and the controversiality of it, that you should definitely check out.

Every math enthusiast, including myself and hopefully you, now, who has encountered this problem has a different opinion on whether or not it's true. This alone is enough to say that math can definitely be controversial and interpretive, adding to the reasons that math is a beautiful thing.

Are other things also more beautiful than they seem?

This is a great opportunity for you to go out and explore. The doors are open for you to go out and challenge any of your previous thoughts and opinions on everything, and see if you truly hate the things you think you hate, or if you just think you do. Is math really that bad? Is biology really your least favorite subject, or did you just have a bad biology teacher? That's for you to decide.
